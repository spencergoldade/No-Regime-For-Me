## Source texts (how to get plain text)

- **Project Gutenberg (plain text UTF-8):** Federalist Papers (ebook 18), Readings on Fascism and National Socialism (14058), Secret Armies (22682), The Pedigree of Fascism (77090). Download from gutenberg.org.
- **Reference folder epubs:** The Federalist Papers (Goldman ed.), It Can't Happen Here (Lewis), They Thought They Were Free (Mayer). Convert to text locally (Calibre, Pandoc, or e-reader export) for extraction.

## Tips (actionable; no attribution)

1–2 sentences, ~15–40 words. One idea per item.

1. When someone says only I can fix it, be suspicious.
2. Support independent media and local journalism; they are early targets.
3. Vote in every election, including local and midterm.
4. Learn how your local government and school board work.
5. Speak up when you hear dehumanizing language about groups of people.
6. Protect the independence of courts and the rule of law.
7. Don't share content you haven't verified; slow down before you retweet.
8. Join or support groups that defend civil liberties and voting rights.
9. Read primary sources; don't rely only on summaries or pundits.
10. Notice when norms are broken and name it.
11. Build ties with neighbors and community; isolation helps authoritarians.
12. Refuse to treat cruelty as normal or inevitable.
13. Support whistleblowers and a free press.
14. Ask who benefits when people are set against each other.
15. Teach the next generation why checks and balances matter.
16. When leaders attack the press, pay more attention to the press.
17. Defend the independence of election administration.
18. Reject the idea that strength means never admitting error.
19. Value expertise and evidence; question claims that dismiss them.
20. Stay in room when hard conversations happen; don't walk away.
21. Don't let fear of conflict silence you on core principles.
22. Support institutions that hold power accountable.
23. Recognize propaganda: repeated slogans, us vs. them, scapegoating.
24. Demand transparency; secrecy grows where accountability shrinks.
25. Stand with those who are targeted first; they are the canary.
26. Question loyalty tests that replace debate with obedience.
27. Remember that authoritarianism often starts with popular support.
28. Keep a habit of reading history; it repeats when forgotten.
29. Reject the idea that the end justifies any means.
30. Support fact-checkers and investigative journalism with your attention and money.
31. When someone says we need to suspend rights for safety, ask for evidence and limits.
32. Protect the right to protest; it is often the first to be restricted.
33. Don't normalize violence or threats against opponents.
34. Value the peaceful transfer of power; it is not automatic.
35. Learn the warning signs of democratic backsliding.
36. Speak in plain language; don't let euphemisms hide harm.
37. Support civic education in schools and in public life.
38. When institutions are weakened, strengthen the ones you can.
39. Don't wait for a single big moment; small actions add up.
40. Listen to people who have lived under authoritarian regimes.
41. Reject the framing that you must choose between security and freedom without evidence.
42. Hold leaders to their oaths and the law.
43. Support international alliances that reinforce democratic norms.
44. Notice when language shifts from debate to elimination.
45. Defend the independence of the civil service.
46. Don't let fatigue or despair stop you from participating.
47. Question anyone who says they alone speak for the people.
48. Protect the right to vote and make it easier for eligible voters to use it.
49. When truth is under attack, share and cite reliable sources.
50. Remember that complacency is a choice; so is resistance.

## Insights (observations; no attribution)

1–2 sentences, ~15–40 words.

51. Authoritarianism often advances in small steps that seem minor at the time.
52. It can't happen here is the thought that makes it possible.
53. Demagogues need enemies; they create them when they don't exist.
54. The state that puts itself above the individual is the one to watch.
55. Ordinary people accommodate tyranny by deciding it doesn't affect them yet.
56. First they came is a warning, not a fate; you can refuse to be silent.
57. Propaganda works by repetition and by shutting out other voices.
58. When the press is called the enemy, the next step is to treat it that way.
59. Checks and balances exist because power corrupts; they must be used.
60. Complacency is the soil in which authoritarianism grows.
61. Strongmen promise order; they deliver dependence and fear.
62. The erosion of norms often precedes the erosion of laws.
63. Scapegoating unifies some by destroying others; reject the bargain.
64. Vigilance is not paranoia; history shows why it is necessary.
65. Liberty requires both the will and the means to defend it.
66. Those who say we must not politicize something are often protecting power.
67. The rule of law protects the weak from the strong.
68. When loyalty to a leader replaces loyalty to the constitution, democracy is at risk.
69. Disinformation aims to confuse so that people give up on truth.
70. Institutions outlast individuals; that is why they are attacked.
71. The promise of safety in exchange for freedom is usually a trap.
72. Small compromises of principle prepare the way for large ones.
73. A free society depends on citizens who act like it.
74. The phrase we have no choice is often the moment when choice still exists.
75. Authoritarians need enablers more than they need true believers.
76. History does not repeat exactly, but it rhymes; learn the patterns.
77. When criticism is called treason, criticism is what is needed most.
78. The separation of powers is there for when one branch fails.
79. Rights that are not exercised can be taken for granted and then taken away.
80. Propaganda targets emotion first; reason and facts are secondary.
81. The normalization of the unacceptable is how the unacceptable becomes law.
82. No one person should hold unchecked power; that is the lesson of tyranny.
83. Solidarity across groups makes authoritarian divide-and-rule harder.
84. The first duty of a citizen in a free society is to protect that freedom.
85. When truth is contested, the contest itself is a form of power.
86. Fear is a tool; don't let it replace your judgment.
87. Democratic backsliding is often popular before it is irreversible.
88. The press that holds power to account is not the enemy of the people.
89. Rights are not gifts from the state; they are limits on it.
90. Those who ignore history are not doomed to repeat it; everyone is, if we forget.
91. Complacency and resistance are both choices; only one preserves freedom.
92. The strongman's promise of order is the prelude to disorder he will blame on others.
93. When institutions are politicized, the politicizers benefit; the public does not.
94. Eternal vigilance is not a slogan; it is a practice.
95. The moment you think it cannot happen here is when you stop guarding against it.
96. Dehumanizing language is the first step toward dehumanizing treatment.
97. Power that is not checked does not stay in good hands for long.
98. A republic requires more than elections; it requires a culture that values freedom.
99. The abuse of power often begins with the abuse of language.
100. No regime for me is a choice you make every day, in small ways.
101. Freedom is not the default; it is maintained by effort and care.
102. When people stop believing they can change things, authoritarians win.
103. The goal of propaganda is not to convince but to confuse and exhaust.
104. Stand for something; neutrality in the face of tyranny helps the tyrant.
105. The past is not past; it informs the present if we let it.
106. Resist the urge to normalize what is not normal.
107. One person speaking up can make it easier for others to follow.
108. The rule of law is what stands between democracy and dictatorship.
109. Question authority; it is the duty of a free citizen.
110. Small acts of resistance and solidarity add up; do not underestimate them.
